Inclined Plane

Real World Applications

Wheelchair ramps, loading docks, mountain roads/switchbacks, slides, conveyor belts, and stairs. A longer, gentler ramp requires less force to move an object upward.

Levers

Real World Applications

Class 1: Seesaw, crowbar, scissors, pliers.
Class 2: Wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener.
Class 3: Fishing rod, tweezers, human forearm.

Gears

Real World Applications

Bicycle gears, clock mechanisms, car transmissions, windmills, industrial machinery. Gears trade speed for torque or vice versa.

Pulleys

Real World Applications

Elevators, cranes, flagpoles, sailing rigging, rock climbing, window blinds. More pulleys = less effort but more rope to pull.

Buoyancy

Real World Applications

Ships float because their average density (hull + air) is less than water. Submarines control ballast tanks. Hot air balloons use heated air (lower density than surrounding air).

Density Layers

Oil: 800 kg/m3 (yellow)
Water: 1000 kg/m3 (blue)
Honey: 1400 kg/m3 (amber)

Real World Applications

Oil spills float on water. Blood tests use density gradients to separate components. Geological layers form by density. Lava lamps use density differences.

Wedge

Real World Applications

Axes, knives, chisels, door stops, nails, plows. A thinner wedge has higher MA, splitting with less force over a longer distance. Essentially a moving inclined plane.

Screw

Real World Applications

Wood screws, bolts, jar lids, car jacks, Archimedes screw (water pump), vises, drill bits. A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

Wheel & Axle

Real World Applications

Steering wheels, door knobs, screwdrivers, ferris wheels, windlass (well crank), bicycle wheels. Force applied at the wheel's edge is multiplied at the axle.
