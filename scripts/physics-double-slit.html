▶ 1. The Setup
An electron gun fires particles one at a time toward a barrier with two narrow slits. Behind the barrier, a detection screen records where each particle lands.

Each electron is a single, indivisible particle. It can only go through one slit... right?

The question: When we fire thousands of individual electrons, what pattern builds up on the screen?
▶ 2. Classical Expectation
If electrons are particles (tiny bullets), each one goes through one slit or the other. We'd expect two bands — one behind each slit.

Think of it this way: throw a thousand tennis balls at a wall with two doorways. You get two piles on the other side. Nothing in between. Nothing weird.

Try it: Select "Classical" mode above and fire. Two bands. Makes sense.
▶ 3. The Shocking Result
Switch to "Unobserved" mode. Fire one electron at a time. Each one hits the screen at a seemingly random spot.

But after hundreds of electrons... an interference pattern appears. Alternating bright and dark bands. This is what waves do — not particles.

The implication is staggering: Each individual electron somehow went through both slits simultaneously and interfered with itself. A single particle behaved like a wave.

This is not a statistical effect. It's not about electrons interacting with each other (they're fired one at a time). Each electron, individually, is a wave spread across both slits until it hits the screen.
▶ 4. The Observer Effect
Now use the OBSERVER TOGGLE above. Turn it ON while particles are firing. Don't reset — just flip the switch.

The interference pattern stops forming. New particles land in two simple bands. The electron now behaves like a particle, going through one slit or the other.

Turn observation OFF again. The interference pattern starts building back up. The electron "knows" when it's being watched.

This is not a metaphor. This is what physically happens. The mere presence of a detector — even one that could theoretically detect without disturbing the electron — destroys the wave behavior.

The act of gaining information about which slit changed reality itself.
▶ 5. What Does This Mean?
Before measurement, the particle exists in superposition — it is in all possible states simultaneously. It doesn't have a definite position. It isn't at slit A or slit B. It's at both. And neither.

Observation forces it to "choose." The wave function collapses into a single definite state.

This raises the deepest question in physics: What counts as an "observation"? Does it require consciousness? A machine? Any interaction with matter?

After a century of debate, physicists still disagree.
▶ 6. The Wave Function
The wave function ψ (psi) describes the probability amplitude of a quantum system.

Probability = |ψ(x)|²

For the double slit:
ψ = A·sin(kr₁ - ωt) + A·sin(kr₂ - ωt)

where r₁ and r₂ are the distances from each slit to point x on the screen.

Constructive interference: Waves align → |ψ|² is large → bright band
Destructive interference: Waves cancel → |ψ|² is zero → dark band

Toggle "Show wave function" above to see the ripples from each slit.
▶ 7. Interpretations
Copenhagen (Bohr, 1927):
The wave function is real. It collapses on measurement. Reality is fundamentally probabilistic. "Don't ask what the electron is doing between measurements — the question has no meaning."

Many-Worlds (Everett, 1957):
The wave function never collapses. Every measurement splits the universe. The electron goes through both slits — in different branches of reality. All outcomes happen.

Pilot Wave (de Broglie-Bohm):
The particle IS a particle — always. But it's guided by a real physical wave. The wave goes through both slits; the particle follows the wave. Deterministic, but nonlocal.

Consciousness Causes Collapse (Wigner, von Neumann):
Only a conscious observer collapses the wave function. Controversial. Most physicists now favor decoherence — interaction with any macroscopic system is sufficient.
▶ 8. Real Experiments
1801 — Thomas Young: Light through two slits produces interference bands. Proves light is a wave.

1927 — Davisson & Germer: Electrons diffract off a crystal. Confirms de Broglie: matter has wave properties.

1961 — Claus Jönsson: First true double-slit with electrons. Interference pattern confirmed.

1989 — Akira Tonomura: Single electrons fired one at a time. The interference pattern builds up dot by dot. Voted "the most beautiful experiment in physics."

1999 — Markus Arndt: Buckminsterfullerene molecules (C₆₀, 720 atoms!) show interference. Even "large" objects are waves.

2019 — Fein et al.: Molecules of 2,000 atoms showed interference. The boundary between quantum and classical keeps moving.
▶ 9. The Delayed Choice
Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment (1978, confirmed 2007):

You can decide whether to observe after the particle has already passed through the slits. The result still matches your choice.

If you choose to look: particle behavior. If you choose not to look: wave behavior. Even though the particle already "decided" at the slits.

The particle's past behavior seems to change based on a future decision.

This doesn't mean you can send messages backward in time. But it does mean that the concept of "what happened" at the slits has no meaning until the measurement is complete.

"The past has no existence except as recorded in the present."
— John Archibald Wheeler
