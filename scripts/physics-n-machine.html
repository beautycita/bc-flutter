How It Works

What is it?

The Faraday disc is the simplest possible electric generator. A conducting disc rotates in a magnetic field, and voltage appears between the center axis and the outer rim. Michael Faraday built the first one in 1831, making it one of the earliest generators ever constructed.

How does it work?

Free electrons in the spinning disc experience a Lorentz force (F = qv x B) that pushes them radially. This charge separation creates an EMF between center and rim:

ε = ½ ω B R²

Where ω is angular velocity, B is magnetic field strength, and R is disc radius. Brush contacts at the axis and rim collect this voltage to power an external circuit.

The Controversy

Bruce DePalma (1935-1997) claimed his version, the "N-Machine," could produce more electrical energy output than the mechanical energy input required to spin the disc — so-called "over-unity" or free energy.

He reported efficiencies of 200-500% in some experiments, asserting the excess energy was extracted from the "primordial energy field of space itself."

Mainstream Physics View

Conventional physics explains all observed outputs through:

  • Back-EMF: When current flows, it creates opposing torque (Lenz's law), requiring more motor power
  • Measurement errors: Homopolar generators produce very high current at very low voltage, making precise power measurements difficult
  • Ohmic losses: Internal resistance of the disc, brushes, and connections dissipate energy as heat
  • No independent replication of over-unity claims has been achieved under controlled conditions
DePalma's View

DePalma argued that rotation affects the inertial properties of matter at a fundamental level. His "spinning ball experiment" (1977) claimed to show that rotating objects fall faster than non-rotating ones.

He theorized the N-Machine tapped into a "vacuum energy" or "zero-point energy" — the quantum mechanical energy that exists in apparently empty space. He viewed rotation as a gateway to accessing this energy.

His supporters included Paramahamsa Tewari (Indian nuclear engineer) who built similar devices and reported similar results.

Historical Context
  • 1831: Faraday invents the homopolar generator
  • 1889: Tesla patents improved homopolar designs
  • 1903: Trouton-Noble experiment (related electrodynamics)
  • 1977: DePalma's spinning ball experiment
  • 1978-85: DePalma builds N-Machine prototypes at MIT
  • 1986: Tewari builds Space Power Generator in India
  • 1997: DePalma dies in New Zealand
Real-World Applications

Homopolar generators (without free-energy claims) are used in:

  • Railguns: Military electromagnetic launchers use massive homopolar generators for megaampere current pulses
  • Metal welding: Inertia welding systems use homopolar generator energy storage
  • Scientific instruments: Plasma physics experiments, MHD generators
  • Industrial: Electroplating, aluminum smelting (high-current, low-voltage applications)
