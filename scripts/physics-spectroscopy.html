Which element produces this spectrum?
Absorption Spectrum: When white light passes through a cool gas, atoms absorb photons at specific wavelengths matching their electron transitions. The result: dark lines on a continuous rainbow. This is how Fraunhofer first observed hundreds of dark lines in sunlight in 1814.

Real-World Applications of Spectroscopy

Stellar Composition

Every element absorbs unique wavelengths. By analyzing starlight, we determine what stars are made of without ever visiting them. The Sun's spectrum reveals hydrogen, helium, iron, calcium, sodium, and dozens more elements.

Redshift & the Expanding Universe

When a star moves away from us, its spectral lines shift toward red (longer wavelengths). Edwin Hubble used this to prove the universe is expanding. The faster a galaxy recedes, the greater the redshift — leading to the discovery of the Big Bang.

Chemical Analysis

Forensics uses spectroscopy to identify unknown substances. Environmental agencies monitor pollution. Food safety labs detect contaminants. Each material has a unique spectral fingerprint that cannot be faked.

Medical Applications

Pulse oximeters use spectroscopy to measure blood oxygen by shining light through your finger. Different hemoglobin states absorb different wavelengths. MRI spectroscopy maps brain chemistry. Blood tests use spectrophotometry.

Astronomy

Beyond composition, spectroscopy reveals stellar temperature (hotter stars peak at bluer wavelengths), rotation speed (line broadening), magnetic fields (Zeeman splitting), and even the presence of exoplanet atmospheres.

Historical: Fraunhofer (1814)

Joseph von Fraunhofer mapped 574 dark lines in the solar spectrum, naming the strongest A through K. He had no idea what caused them. Decades later, Kirchhoff and Bunsen proved each line corresponds to a specific chemical element.
